Mountain village to IIT: the electricity gap

Shubhankar grew up in a village where electricity was barely available. This wasn't a motivational-poster childhood — it was material constraint. He made it to IIT Mumbai, a filter school. That alone is rare. But becoming an entrepreneur at IIT was the real pivot point. The gap between where he started and where institutions like IIT demanded you perform wasn't just academic. It was about access itself.

Becoming an accidental entrepreneur while still an undergrad

He founded a venture while at IIT — the description suggests it was high-risk, high-learning. By the time he sat for his GMAT attempts, he had real entrepreneurial scars: what worked, what didn't, where he lacked skills. That experience wasn't a sidecar to GMAT prep. It was the reason he had the discipline to execute a perfect score.

Why 800 GMAT mattered more than just a score

A perfect GMAT score from someone coming out of a startup isn't just test mastery. It signals: founder-level discipline, English fluency from a zero baseline, ability to execute flawlessly under pressure. Stanford saw 800 and saw someone who'd already failed and learned. The score was proof that he could build something complex and bring it to completion, even in a domain (standardized testing) that wasn't his native strength.

What changes when you walk the halls of change-makers

Shubhankar entered Stanford with classmates who'd worked at Netflix, Google, LinkedIn before their MBAs. The institution's stated mission is "change lives, organizations, the world." For someone from a village with no power, this cohort density and mission clarity is not background — it's the entire difference. The people in the room weren't there to optimize salary or resume. They were already founders, executives, builders. That's the accelerator his GMAT score unlocked.
