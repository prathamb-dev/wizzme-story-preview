How Subham went from 710 to 760 by restarting with structure

One working professional's 10-month transformation: what changed between a 15-day panic attempt and a 760 score wasn't willpower — it was preparation discipline.

Before
Rushed 710 attempt with 15 days of mock practice while joining Westbridge; thought there was no time for GMAT
→
After
Committed 10-month GMAT prep cycle ending in 760; structured partnership with coach; joined MBA program

Why Subham's first attempt was always going to fail

Subham's first GMAT attempt came with 15 days of mock practice — after he'd already joined Westbridge (an investment firm). He convinced himself there was no time for prep. The 710 score wasn't surprising; it was inevitable. A 15-day attempt, even if you ace the mocks, doesn't build the sustained skill required. It's a diagnostic, not a legitimate effort. The real learning came from accepting this, not from beating himself up.

The critical decision: waiting for the right moment to try again

After the first attempt, Subham didn't immediately retake. His job had started. He let GMAT sit. This is the counter-intuitive part of the story — the win came from not panicking into a second rushed attempt. It came from waiting until October 2021, nearly a year later, when he was ready to commit properly. That patience wasn't procrastination. It was accepting that he wasn't ready yet, and would only be wasting money and time on another 15-day panic sprint.

How a coach became the accountability system that worked

Subham reached out to a GMAT coach (Swathaman) in October 2021 — 10 months before his 760. The coach wasn't just teaching; he was building a tracking system, milestone reviews, and strategic weak-area work. This replaced the "mock practice" model that had failed before. Week-to-week visibility meant Subham could adjust based on data, not on how motivated he felt on Tuesday morning. The coach became the external structure that working professionals need when life keeps trying to interrupt prep.

The score came from consistency, not cramming

Over 10 months with a coach, hitting specific milestones, Subham's prep was visible week-to-week. The 760 wasn't a surprise at the end — it was the inevitable result of sustained, coached discipline. The jump from 710 to 760 (50 points) looks big, but it came from systematic skill-building, not late-night panic work. When you build the system first and the score follows, you don't just get a higher number. You get the confidence that you can execute long-term commitments even when life gets messy.
