Arjun graduated from IIT KGP in 2022 with a Mechanical Engineering degree and exactly zero formal product experience. By December of that year, he had an offer from Growfast, a Series B startup. What happened between those two points is less a story of talent and more a story of learning to ask the right questions.

"The first five companies rejected me in the first round," he told Riya. "And I kept thinking the problem was my resume. I spent weeks polishing it. But then someone who'd been through it told me — nobody cares about your resume at the screening stage. They care if you can think like a user."

"Most IIT students walk into PM interviews solving for the product. The interviewer is waiting for you to ask 'who is this for?' That shift — from feature-thinker to user-advocate — was the only thing that actually moved the needle for me." — Arjun Mehta, 14:32 into the conversation

The turning point came from an unlikely direction. Arjun had been helping a senior PM on LinkedIn with feedback on a side project — with no agenda beyond genuine interest. Three weeks later, that same PM mentioned an opening at Growfast. The "unofficial interview" that followed — a 45-minute WhatsApp call — prepared him better than any mock session had.

When the formal process began, Arjun used the CIRCLES framework for every product-sense case. Not because it was novel, but because it was consistent. "Interviewers want structure. They're evaluating your thinking process, not whether you came up with a brilliant feature." He cleared three rounds. Then came the offer — ₹12 LPA. He had no competing bid. He still pushed.

His negotiation was simple: he framed the ask around market data, not desperation. "I said I was very excited about the role, and I'd researched PM comp at Series B companies in Bangalore — most were in the ₹16–18 range. I asked if there was flexibility." The offer came back at ₹16.8 LPA. He had a job.

Act 1
The Why

Sneha graduated from VIT Vellore in 2022 with a CS degree and a job offer at a mid-sized IT services company. The role was fine. The work was fine. Six months in, she realized "fine" was not something she could do for the next decade.

"Everyone from my college either goes into service companies or tries for a product startup. I wanted neither. I wanted to actually understand how businesses work — not just build features for someone else's vision."

"MBA felt like the only legitimate path to reset your trajectory in India without a pedigree. I knew that. But I also knew IIM from a tier 3 college is supposed to be almost impossible. That almost is the operative word."
Act 2
Attempt 1 — The Honest Account

First attempt. Eight months of self-study, no coaching, YouTube and free material. She scored 94 percentile — a number most people would celebrate. But 94 from VIT doesn't get IIM calls. The shortlisting process factors in academic profile and work experience. Tier 3 college with a low CGPA is a double penalty.

"I didn't get a single IIM call. Not even the newer ones. I sat with that result for two weeks before I decided to go again."

Act 3
The Reset

She went back and audited exactly where her score broke down. LRDI — Logical Reasoning and Data Interpretation — was at 72nd percentile. Everything else was above 90. One section was costing her everything.

"I'd been studying all three sections equally for 8 months. That was the mistake. I needed 99 overall, not 90 in three sections. The maths of percentiles is brutal — one weak section tanks you. I spent the first 3 months of attempt 2 doing nothing but LRDI."

The other change: she built a prep group with two others also doing their second attempt. Three people, daily check-ins, shared mock analysis. "Having people who understood the weight of a second attempt was the only thing that kept me going in month 4."

Act 4
Attempt 2 — The Number

CAT day. She'd done 24 full mocks. Her LRDI was consistently 95th+ percentile in the last 8 mocks. She walked in knowing her section-by-section strategy, her time splits, exactly when to skip and move on.

"I was calm in a way I wasn't in attempt 1. Not because I was confident — I was terrified. But I had a plan for every scenario. That's different."

Act 5
Converting IIM Calcutta

Three calls — IIM Calcutta, IIM Lucknow, IIM Kozhikode. The GD/PI process was where the tier 3 background became the real test. Interviewers explicitly asked about it. "Why VIT? Why low CGPA? What makes you think you belong here?"

"I'd spent time building a narrative that didn't apologize for where I came from. I explained my choices, what I learned, what I built despite the environment. Not excuses — a story with a coherent arc."

"The IIM Calcutta interviewer asked me something I wasn't prepared for: 'What would you have done differently?' I said I would have started CAT prep in my 2nd year of college. She paused and said that was the most honest answer she'd heard all day."
What I'd tell you
01
Find your weak section and go all in on it. Studying everything equally sounds logical but it's the wrong strategy if you need 99+. One section tanks you disproportionately.
02
A second attempt is psychologically harder, not practically. You'll feel like everyone knows you failed once. They don't. IIM doesn't care. Your number is your number.
03
Build your PI narrative before you start prep, not after results. Your background, your choices, your gaps — all of it needs a coherent story. Not excuses. An arc.
04
Find 2-3 people doing the same journey. Not for motivation — for mock analysis. Having people who can tell you exactly where your LRDI broke down is worth more than any coaching material.
